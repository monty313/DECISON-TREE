
Momentum trading is fundamentally about identifying when a fast-moving "orbit" (short-period indicator) aligns with the pull of a slower, more massive "gravity center" (long-period indicator) across multiple timeframes — and then exploiting that alignment before it decays. I'll teach this the way your system's own trading laws define it, using the IRAC method (Issue, Rule, Application, Conclusion) so the logic is transferable to any indicator combo.

The Gravity Principle Explained
Every trend indicator acts like a mass — the longer the period, the heavier the gravitational pull, and price/faster indicators "orbit" around it. Oscillators measure deviation from equilibrium (RSI 50, CCI 0), and when that deviation stretches too far from the gravity center without support, it snaps back — that's mean reversion. A "slingshot" happens when the fast orbit (short MA, fast CCI) accelerates in the same direction as the slow gravity (long MA, slow CCI), compounding momentum instead of fighting it.

IRAC Framework for Teaching Momentum Identification
Issue: How do you know if momentum is real and tradeable versus noise or chop?

Rule: Per the trading laws, every regime must be classified into a gravity state (Bull/Bear/Neutral) and a volatility state (Nothing Happening/Tradable/Great Movement) before any entry is considered. No new momentum trades are permitted in a Neutral gravity or a "Nothing Happening" volatility state.

Application: Check higher-timeframe (HTF) trend gravity first — e.g., is price above both SMA200 and BB200-middle on H1? That sets bias. Then confirm volatility is expanding (ADX above its shifted SMA, ATR above its shifted SMA) — this is the "Great Movement" filter that separates tradeable trends from dead chop.

Conclusion: Only classify momentum as tradeable when HTF gravity direction, LTF trigger, and volatility expansion all agree simultaneously — this triple-confirmation is what the framework calls a "Super" signal.

Taking Advantage of Pullbacks vs High-Momentum Entries
There are two distinct entry philosophies, and mixing them without discipline is the most common execution error.

Pullback entries (mean-reversion within trend): In an up-regime (fast MA above slow MA), buy when price dips to the fast MA or when a fast oscillator (RSI5) crosses back above 50 — this is buying the retrace to the gravity line, not the extreme.

High-momentum "Super" entries: Reserved for when HTF and LTF are both fully aligned and volatility is expanding — these are breakout/outbreak entries where price or an oscillator pierces its own volatility band (Bollinger on CCI, or price beyond BB upper).

Exit discipline for pullbacks: Close when the oscillator completes its round-trip (RSI5 hits 40 on a long) or price crosses back through the mean MA.

Exit discipline for momentum entries: Trail using the mid-speed indicator flip (CCI100/SMA crossing zero against you) rather than the fast one, to avoid getting shaken out early.

Risk sizing rule that applies to both: Never risk more than 1% per trade, and never scale in against the dominant gravity direction, regardless of how attractive the counter-move looks.

The single most important generalizable principle here: classify regime first (gravity + volatility), then let the timeframe hierarchy — not the fastest indicator alone — decide both entry and exit. This is what prevents an agent from chasing every crossover and instead trades only genuine, conserved-momentum opportunities.
